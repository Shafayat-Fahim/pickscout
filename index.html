Robotics Wiki

Robotics: machines that sense, decide, and act

Robotics combines mechanical design, electronics, control systems, sensors, software, and artificial intelligence to build machines that can perform useful work. Robots may be teleoperated by people, run from programmed routines, or make autonomous decisions from sensor data.

Industrial Robots

Factory automation and precision handling

Industrial robots are fixed or semi-fixed machines used for repetitive, high-speed, or hazardous work such as welding, painting, sorting, pick-and-place, packaging, and CNC machine tending.

Examples: articulated robot arms, SCARA robots, delta robots, collaborative robots, automated guided vehicles in warehouses.

Service Robots

Robots that assist people in daily tasks

Service robots work outside traditional factories. They support hospitality, hospitals, agriculture, homes, education, delivery, cleaning, inspection, and public services.

Examples: delivery robots, robotic vacuum cleaners, hotel room-service robots, hospital logistics carts, crop-monitoring field robots.

Social Robots

Designed for interaction, trust, and communication

Social robots are built to communicate with people through speech, gestures, facial expressions, screens, or body movement. Their design focuses on human-robot interaction as much as mechanics.

Examples: Pepper-style reception robots, classroom assistants, therapy companions, museum guide robots, research robots for emotion and dialogue.

Mobile Robots

Moving through rooms, roads, fields, and planets

Mobile robots use wheels, tracks, legs, propellers, or hybrid motion systems to travel through an environment. Navigation depends on odometry, maps, cameras, lidar, radar, GPS, and obstacle detection.

Examples: warehouse AMRs, Mars rovers, drones, inspection crawlers, agricultural field robots, autonomous delivery vehicles.

Autonomous Robots

Perceive, plan, and act with limited supervision

Autonomous robots make decisions from sensor input instead of following only direct human commands. Their software usually includes perception, localization, planning, control, and safety monitoring.

Examples: self-driving delivery robots, warehouse picking robots, autonomous drones, underwater survey robots, robotic lawn mowers.

Humanoid Robots

Humanlike form for human spaces

Humanoid robots use a body plan inspired by people: head, torso, arms, hands, and sometimes legs. The shape helps them use human tools, navigate human spaces, and communicate naturally.

Examples: Ameca, Atlas-style research robots, biped walking platforms, humanoid reception robots, dexterous manipulation research systems.

Surgical Robots

High-precision tools for medical teams

Surgical robots help clinicians perform delicate procedures through small incisions, often by translating a surgeon's hand movements into scaled, tremor-filtered instrument motion.

Examples: da Vinci-style telemanipulators, robotic endoscope holders, orthopedic positioning robots, microsurgery systems, rehabilitation exoskeletons.

Rescue Robots

Machines for dangerous search and response

Rescue robots enter places that may be unsafe for humans, such as collapsed buildings, fires, flooded areas, chemical incidents, mines, or bomb-disposal zones.

Examples: tracked search robots, EOD manipulators, disaster drones, snake robots for rubble gaps, underwater rescue ROVs.

Robot Types at a Glance

A quick comparison of where each family is usually used and what design challenge matters most.

Robot Family Typical Workspace Main Strength Key Challenge
Industrial Factories and warehouses Speed, repeatability, payload Safety around people and flexible tasks
Service Homes, hospitals, hotels, public spaces Human assistance and logistics Unstructured environments
Social Schools, care centers, reception areas Communication and engagement Trust, privacy, and natural interaction
Mobile and autonomous Roads, sidewalks, fields, labs Navigation and independent operation Reliable perception in changing conditions
Surgical and rescue Hospitals and hazardous sites Precision or remote presence Fail-safe control and operator confidence
